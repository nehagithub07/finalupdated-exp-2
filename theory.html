To study the Load Characteristics of DC shunt generator

Introduction

A DC shunt generator is a type of self-excited DC generator in which the field winding is connected in parallel (shunt) with the armature winding. As a result, the field current remains almost constant (since it depends only on the terminal voltage). The yoke of a DC generator is made of cast iron because it provides high magnetic permeability, good mechanical strength, and serves as a low-reluctance path for the magnetic flux.

The performance of a DC shunt generator is described using load characteristics, which illustrate how the terminal voltage and load current change as the load varies.

In a shunt generator, the field winding is connected in parallel with the armature winding so that the terminal voltage of the generator is applied across it. The shunt field winding has many turns of fine wire having high resistance. Therefore, only a part of the armature current flows through the shunt field winding, and the rest flows through the load. Fig. 1 shows the connections of a shunt wound generator. The armature current Ia splits up into two parts - a small fraction Ish flowing through the shunt field winding, while the major part IL goes to the external load.

Fig. 1 Equivalent circuit of DC shunt generator for load characteristics

Losses in a DC Shunt Generator

In a DC shunt generator, various losses occur during operation, such as armature copper losses, shunt field copper losses, iron losses, and mechanical losses. Among these, armature copper losses constitute a significant portion of the total losses. The copper losses in the armature of a DC shunt generator are approximately 30% of the total full-load losses. These losses occur due to the resistance of the armature winding and increase with load current, thereby affecting the efficiency and voltage regulation of the generator.

Internal characteristic

The internal characteristic curve represents the relation between the generated voltage Eg and the load current IL. When the generator is loaded then the generated voltage decreases due to armature reaction. So, generated voltage becomes lower than the emf generated at no load. External or Load characteristic

AC curve shows the external characteristic of the shunt wound DC generators. It shows the variation of terminal voltage with the load current. Ohmic drop due to armature resistance gives lesser terminal voltage than the generated voltage. That is why the curve lies below the internal characteristic curve.

V = (Eg − IaRa) = Eg − (IL + Ish)Ra

The terminal voltage can always be maintained constant by adjusting the load terminal. When the load resistance of a shunt-wound DC generator is decreased, the load current increases as shown in the above figure. But the load current can only be increased up to a certain limit (up to point C) with decrease of load resistance. Beyond this point, the characteristic reverses. Any further decrease of load resistance results in current reduction and consequently, the external characteristic curve turns back as shown in the dotted line, and ultimately the terminal voltage becomes zero (though some voltage remains due to residual magnetism).

When IL increases, terminal voltage decreases. After a certain limit, due to heavy load current and increased ohmic drop, the terminal voltage reduces drastically. This drastic reduction results in a drop in load current even though load resistance becomes low. Therefore, the load resistance of the machine must be maintained properly. The point at which the machine gives maximum current output is called the breakdown point (point C in the figure).

Note: It may be seen from the external characteristic that change in terminal voltage from no-load to full-load is small. The terminal voltage can always be maintained constant by adjusting the field rheostat R automatically.
